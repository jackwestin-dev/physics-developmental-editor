Newton's Third Law
Imagine you are standing on a skateboard near a wall. You reach out and push against the wall with both hands. The wall doesn't move, but you do. Without taking a single step, without pushing against the floor, you glide smoothly backward the moment your hands make contact with the wall. Something pushed you. But what?
This scenario contains the essence of Newton's Third Law of Motion. The wall pushed you at the same moment you pushed it, and with the same strength. You just weren't paying attention to the force acting on you, because you were focusing on the force you were applying to the wall.
Newton's Third Law of Motion states that whenever one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts a force back on the first that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. These two forces always act on different objects and always occur simultaneously.
We represent Newton's Third Law mathematically as:
FAB=-FBA
The subscripts follow the same on/by convention you have been using throughout this chapter: FAB is the force exerted on object A by object B, and FBA is the force exerted on object B by object A. The negative sign tells you that these two forces point in opposite directions while always remaining equal in magnitude.
The two forces in this equation are commonly referred to as an action-reaction pair: one force is called the action, and the other is called the reaction. These labels, however, are nothing more than names. Either force can be called the action; whichever one you label the action, the other is then the reaction. More importantly, the labels imply no sequence and no cause and effect. The two forces do not take turns, i.e., the action does not happen first and then trigger the reaction. Both forces come into existence at the instant the two objects begin interacting and disappear at the instant the interaction ends. They are simultaneous by nature.
Identifying Action-Reaction Pairs: A Closer Look
This raises a natural question: what exactly qualifies two forces as an action-reaction pair? To answer it carefully, consider a box resting at rest on a table.
Two forces act on the box. Earth pulls the box downward with a gravitational force, and the table pushes the box upward with a normal force. These two forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction; you can confirm this immediately from Newton's Second Law, since the box is not accelerating. When you see this for the first time, you might conclude that these two forces must therefore be an action-reaction pair. They are not, and understanding why is essential.

Caption: The two forces acting on the box are the gravitational force from Earth and the normal force from the table. These two forces act on the same object and are therefore not an action-reaction pair.
For two forces to form a valid action-reaction pair, they must satisfy two conditions simultaneously:
The two forces must act on different objects.
Both forces currently in the box's free-body diagram — the gravitational pull from Earth and the normal push from the table — act on the box. Their equality here is a consequence of equilibrium, not of Newton's Third Law. Since both forces act on the same object, they fail this first condition immediately and cannot be an action-reaction pair, regardless of anything else.
The two forces must arise from the same interaction between the same two objects.
To form a valid pair, the two forces must be each other's direct response to a single interaction, i.e., the same two objects acting on each other through the same type of force.
To find the true action-reaction pairs that satisfy these two conditions, you need to:
Look at each interaction between two objects separately, and then,
Ask: What force does each object exert on the other?
Let’s put this into action. The first interaction is between the box and Earth. Earth pulls the box downward with a gravitational force. That is one force. By Newton's Third Law, the box must pull Earth upward with a gravitational force of the same magnitude. That is the paired force.
These two forces involve the same two objects, arise from the same field interaction (the gravitational force in this case), and act on different objects: one on the box, the other on Earth. They are a valid action-reaction pair.

Caption: The gravitational interaction between the box and Earth produces a valid action-reaction pair. Each force acts on a different object.
The second interaction is between the box and the table. The table pushes the box upward with a normal force. That is one force. By Newton's Third Law, the box must push the table downward with a normal force of the same magnitude.
These two forces involve the same two objects, arise from the same contact interaction (the normal force in this case), and act on different objects: one on the box, one on the table. They are the second valid action-reaction pair.

Caption: The contact interaction between the box and the table produces a second valid action-reaction pair. Again, each force acts on a different object.
Now consider what happens if you suddenly remove the table from beneath the box. The box begins to fall freely. The normal force that the table exerts on the box immediately drops to zero, and so does the box's normal force on the table, because the contact interaction between them ceases. But the gravitational interaction between the box and Earth is entirely unaffected. Earth still pulls the box downward, and the box still pulls Earth upward with the same forces as before.

Caption: When the table is removed, the contact interaction disappears entirely. The gravitational interaction between the box and Earth is unaffected, confirming that the two action-reaction pairs are independent of each other.
This is a useful test: if removing one object causes both forces to vanish together (as we see when the table is removed and both normal forces disappear), they were a true action-reaction pair arising from the same interaction. If removing one object causes only one force to vanish while the other remains (as we see here with the normal force from the table and the gravitational force from Earth), the two forces were never a pair to begin with.
Conclusion: An action-reaction pair is not simply any two forces that are equal and opposite. It is specifically two forces that satisfy both conditions: they act on two different objects and arise from the same interaction between them.
Keeping this in mind will help you identify true action-reaction pairs correctly and avoid misconceptions you might encounter.
