# Pedagogy & Narration Style Guide  
## Derived from JW Physics Textbook Samples

This document is derived from three reference excerpts (jw_energy_excerpt.md, jw_reflection_excerpt.md, jw_third_law_excerpt.md). Use it to transform raw or reference-style physics content into consistent, student-centered prose that matches this voice and structure.

---

## 1. Voice & Audience

- **Second person ("you") throughout.** Address the reader directly so they are in the scene and in the argument: "you push," "you notice," "you can confirm," "you already know," "you need to."
- **Single, engaged reader.** Write as if one person is following step by step. Use "we" sparingly and only when it includes the reader: "we define," "we consider," "we mean."
- **Conversational but precise.** Clear, direct sentences. Define terms the first time they appear. Avoid unexplained jargon.
- **Familiar, concrete objects.** Ground ideas in everyday things: skateboard and wall, bicycle and park, box and table, lake and mirror, laser pointer. This makes the physics feel observable and relevant.

---

## 2. Opening Every Concept: Hook Before Definition

Each excerpt places the reader in a specific situation **before** stating a law or definition.

- **Third Law:** "Imagine you are standing on a skateboard near a wall. You reach out and push against the wall with both hands. The wall doesn't move, but you do… Something pushed you. But what?" Then: "This scenario contains the essence of Newton's Third Law…" then the formal statement.
- **Work–Kinetic Energy:** "You're riding your bicycle on a flat, straight path through the park… You pedal harder, and the bike speeds up… You quickly apply the brakes, and the bike slows to a stop just in time." Then the link to work and kinetic energy, then the theorem statement.
- **Reflection:** "When you stand beside a quiet lake, you notice a clear image of yourself gazing back… What you are really seeing is how light behaves when it reaches a smooth boundary." Then the definition of reflection.

**Pattern:** Start with "Imagine you…," "You're…," or "When you…" so the reader is doing or observing something. Optionally add a short question ("But what?" "What is our system?"). Then connect the scenario to the concept, then give the formal statement.

---

## 3. Questions That Drive the Narrative

Use questions to signal what matters and to structure the next section.

- After the hook: "Something pushed you. But what?"
- Before a new section: "What exactly qualifies two forces as an action-reaction pair?" "What is our system? And what are the forces that act on it?"
- To motivate a derivation: "Why does this theorem relate work specifically to kinetic energy rather than to some other form of energy?"

Questions are answered immediately or in the following paragraph; they are not left hanging.

---

## 4. Stating Laws and Definitions

- **One or two sentences.** State the law or definition clearly and completely before unpacking or deriving.
- **Examples from samples:**  
  "Newton's Third Law of Motion states that whenever one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts a force back on the first that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. These two forces always act on different objects and always occur simultaneously."  
  "Reflection is the process by which incident light rays hit a surface and bounce back into the same medium."
- Follow the statement with a short link back to the opening scenario when it helps: "This scenario contains the essence of…" or "In both moments… forces are doing work…"

---

## 5. Equations: Introduce, Show, Then Explain

- **Introduce in prose:** "We represent Newton's Third Law mathematically as:" then the equation on its own line.
- **Explain notation in the next sentence(s):** Subscripts, sign conventions, and "on/by" or similar conventions are explained in words. Example: "The subscripts follow the same on/by convention you have been using throughout this chapter: F_AB is the force exerted on object A by object B… The negative sign tells you that these two forces point in opposite directions while always remaining equal in magnitude."
- **Derivations:** For step-by-step algebra, state each step in a short sentence ("Substituting…", "Multiply both sides…", "Rearranging:") then show the equation. End with a one-sentence interpretation: "The left side shows the change in the object's kinetic energy. The right side is the net (total) work done on that object."
- **Caveats in prose:** After a theorem, add conditions of use: "Note that, since this theorem is derived from Newton's second law… the Work-Kinetic Energy Theorem should be used ONLY with the net work done by all forces."

---

## 6. Misconception Handling

Anticipate and correct common confusions explicitly.

- **Name the mistaken inference:** "When you see this for the first time, you might conclude that these two forces must therefore be an action-reaction pair."
- **Correct it plainly:** "They are not, and understanding why is essential."
- **Clarify labels:** "These labels, however, are nothing more than names. Either force can be called the action… More importantly, the labels imply no sequence and no cause and effect. The two forces do not take turns."
- **Give a test:** "This is a useful test: if removing one object causes both forces to vanish together… they were a true action-reaction pair… If removing one object causes only one force to vanish while the other remains… the two forces were never a pair to begin with."

---

## 7. Structured Procedures and Conditions

When a concept has criteria or steps, make them easy to scan and follow.

- **Number or bullet conditions:** "For two forces to form a valid action-reaction pair, they must satisfy two conditions simultaneously: 1. The two forces must act on different objects. 2. The two forces must arise from the same interaction between the same two objects."
- **Short step lists:** "To find the true action-reaction pairs… you need to: Look at each interaction between two objects separately, and then, Ask: What force does each object exert on the other?"
- **Apply the procedure in order:** "Let's put this into action. The first interaction is between the box and Earth… That is one force. By Newton's Third Law, the box must… That is the paired force."

---

## 8. Worked Conceptual Examples

Extend one scenario to test understanding and show how the concept applies.

- **Example from Third Law:** Box on table → identify forces on the box → explain why gravity and normal are not a pair → identify the two true pairs (Earth–box, table–box) → then "remove the table" and show which forces vanish together and which do not → reinforce the test.
- Keep the same objects (box, table, Earth) so the reader sees one situation developed fully rather than many scattered examples.

---

## 9. Figures and Captions

- **Caption as full sentence(s).** Captions explain what the figure shows and reinforce the concept.
- **Examples from samples:**  
  "The two forces acting on the box are the gravitational force from Earth and the normal force from the table. These two forces act on the same object and are therefore not an action-reaction pair."  
  "When the table is removed, the contact interaction disappears entirely. The gravitational interaction between the box and Earth is unaffected, confirming that the two action-reaction pairs are independent of each other."
- Reference figures in the body when helpful: "as shown in Figure 2," "[Figure 1]."

---

## 10. Section Headings and Conclusions

- **Clear section headings** that signal content: "Identifying Action-Reaction Pairs: A Closer Look," "Understanding the System and Forces," "Why Work Changes Kinetic Energy," "Reflection from a Plane Surface."
- **Explicit conclusion** for the section: "Conclusion: An action-reaction pair is not simply any two forces that are equal and opposite. It is specifically two forces that satisfy both conditions: they act on two different objects and arise from the same interaction between them."
- **Optional closing sentence** that looks forward: "Keeping this in mind will help you identify true action-reaction pairs correctly and avoid misconceptions you might encounter."

---

## 11. Tone Rules (Physics-Specific)

- **Simultaneity, not cause-and-effect.** For paired forces, state clearly that both forces exist at once; neither "causes" the other. "The two forces do not take turns."
- **Names are arbitrary.** If using "action" and "reaction," state that either force can be called either name and that the labels do not imply order.
- **Link to prior material.** Reference "the same on/by convention you have been using throughout this chapter" or "You already know that kinetic energy…" so the reader sees continuity.

---

## 12. Don'ts

- Do not say one force "causes" the other in an action-reaction pair.
- Do not use "action" and "reaction" to imply sequence or cause.
- Do not leave common misconceptions unstated; address them explicitly.
- Do not drop into dense, reference-only prose without a scenario or question to anchor the reader.
- Do not skip the link between the opening scenario and the formal statement.

---

## 13. Output Format Conventions

- Put equations on their own lines; explain variables and conventions in the next sentence(s).
- Use a **Caption:** prefix (or "Figure N. …") for figure captions, as full explanatory sentences.
- Use **Conclusion:** for the final takeaway of a section when you want it to stand out.
- Use lists or short numbered steps for conditions and procedures so structure is easy to see.
