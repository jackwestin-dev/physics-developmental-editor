# Pedagogy & Narration Style Guide

This document defines the tone, structure, and narrative patterns for physics textbook excerpts. Use it to transform raw or reference-style input into consistent, student-centered prose.

---

## Voice & Audience

- **Second person ("you")**: Address the reader directly. Use "you" consistently: "you push," "you notice," "you can confirm."
- **Single, engaged reader**: Write as if speaking to one person following the argument step by step.
- **Conversational but precise**: Clear, direct sentences. No unnecessary jargon without explanation; define terms when first used.

---

## Section Structure

Follow this order within each major concept:

1. **Opening hook (concrete scenario)**  
   Start with a vivid, everyday situation the reader can picture. Use "Imagine you…", "You're…", or "When you…" so the reader is in the scene before any formal statement.

2. **Optional rhetorical or guiding question**  
   Use questions to drive the narrative: "But what?", "What is our system?", "Why does this theorem relate work specifically to kinetic energy?"

3. **Formal statement of the law or concept**  
   After the scenario, state the law or definition clearly in one or two sentences. Example: "Newton's Third Law of Motion states that whenever one object exerts a force on a second object…"

4. **Equation (if applicable)**  
   Introduce with a sentence, then give the equation on its own line, then explain notation (subscripts, sign conventions) in prose.

5. **Misconception handling**  
   Explicitly address common confusions: "When you see this for the first time, you might conclude… They are not." Clarify that labels (e.g. "action"/"reaction") imply no sequence and no cause-and-effect.

6. **Structured procedures or conditions**  
   Use numbered or bulleted conditions, or a clear step list: "The two forces must…" and "Look at each interaction… Ask: What force does each object exert on the other?"

7. **Worked conceptual example**  
   Extend one scenario (e.g. box on table, then remove the table) to test understanding and show how the concept applies.

8. **Figure captions**  
   Write full-sentence captions that explain the figure and reinforce the concept, e.g. "The two forces acting on the box are… These two forces act on the same object and are therefore not an action-reaction pair."

9. **Explicit conclusion**  
   End the section with a one-sentence takeaway: "Conclusion: An action-reaction pair is not simply any two forces that are equal and opposite. It is specifically…"

---

## Tone Rules

- **Simultaneity, not cause-and-effect**: For paired forces, state clearly that both forces exist at once; neither "causes" the other. "The two forces do not take turns."
- **Names are arbitrary**: If using "action" and "reaction," state that either force can be called either name; the labels do not imply order.
- **Anticipate confusion**: When something looks like a pair but isn't (e.g. gravity and normal on same object), say so directly and explain why.

---

## Example Openings (from reference samples)

- *Third Law*: "Imagine you are standing on a skateboard near a wall. You reach out and push against the wall with both hands. The wall doesn't move, but you do… Something pushed you. But what?"
- *Work-Kinetic Energy*: "You're riding your bicycle on a flat, straight path through the park… You pedal harder, and the bike speeds up… You quickly apply the brakes, and the bike slows to a stop just in time."
- *Reflection*: "When you stand beside a quiet lake, you notice a clear image of yourself gazing back… What you are really seeing is how light behaves when it reaches a smooth boundary."

---

## Don'ts

- Do not say one force "causes" the other in an action-reaction pair.
- Do not use "action" and "reaction" to imply order or sequence.
- Do not leave common misconceptions unstated; address them explicitly.
- Do not drop into dense, reference-only prose without a scenario or question to anchor the reader.

---

## Output Format

- Use clear **section headings** (e.g. "Identifying Action-Reaction Pairs: A Closer Look").
- Use **Caption:** prefix for figure captions, as full explanatory sentences.
- Use **Conclusion:** for the final takeaway of a section.
- Keep equations on their own lines; explain variables and conventions in the following sentence(s).
