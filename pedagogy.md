# Pedagogy & Narration Style Guide  
## Derived from JW Physics Textbook Samples

This document is derived from three reference excerpts (jw_energy_excerpt.md, jw_reflection_excerpt.md, jw_third_law_excerpt.md). Use it to transform raw or reference-style physics content into consistent, student-centered prose that matches this voice and structure.

---

## 0. Originality Requirements (CRITICAL — read first)

The reference excerpts and the input content are **source material you must not copy**. Your output must be substantially original:

- **Never reproduce sentences or phrases from the input or the reference excerpts.** Absorb the *physics* and the *structural patterns*, then express everything in your own words from scratch. If a sentence in your draft could be found — even approximately — in the input or reference samples, rewrite it until it cannot.
- **Invent entirely new opening scenarios.** If the input uses a bicycle in a park, you must choose a completely different setting, different objects, and different characters or situations. The scenario must still be concrete and everyday, but it must be yours.
- **Create fresh numerical problems and examples.** Never reuse masses, distances, speeds, forces, or other quantities from the input. Choose new realistic values and work through the math with those values. If the input derives an equation step-by-step, you should still show the derivation, but frame it around your new scenario with your new numbers.
- **Rephrase all definitions and law statements.** State the same physics in different words. You may keep standard equation forms (F = ma, KE = ½mv², etc.), but the surrounding prose that introduces, explains, and interprets those equations must be freshly written.
- **Use different everyday objects and analogies.** If the reference samples use a skateboard-and-wall scenario, do not use a skateboard and wall. Pick a different pair of interacting objects. Vary the objects across your output so that it is clearly distinct from the samples.
- **Self-check before finishing:** Re-read your output alongside the input. Any passage that closely echoes the input's wording must be rewritten. The goal is that no paragraph in your output shares more than a few common content words with any paragraph in the input.

## 1. Voice & Audience

- **Second person ("you") throughout.** Address the reader directly so they are in the scene and in the argument.
- **Single, engaged reader.** Write as if one person is following step by step. Use "we" sparingly and only when it includes the reader.
- **Conversational but precise.** Clear, direct sentences. Define terms the first time they appear. Avoid unexplained jargon.
- **Familiar, concrete objects.** Ground ideas in everyday things the reader can picture — but always choose objects and settings that are **different from those used in the input and reference samples**. This makes the physics feel observable and relevant while keeping the output original.

---

## 2. Opening Every Concept: Hook Before Definition

Each section places the reader in a specific, concrete situation **before** stating a law or definition.

**Pattern (follow the structure, not the specific wording or scenarios from the samples):**
1. Open with "Imagine you…," "You're…," or "When you…" so the reader is doing or observing something in an everyday setting.
2. Optionally pose a short question that creates curiosity about what just happened.
3. Connect the scenario to the concept you are about to teach.
4. State the formal definition or law.

**Important:** You must invent a *brand-new* scenario for every concept. Do not reuse any scenario, setting, or object combination from the input or from the reference excerpts.

---

## 3. Questions That Drive the Narrative

Use questions to signal what matters and to structure the next section.

- After the hook, pose a question that highlights the surprising or puzzling element of the scenario.
- Before a new section, ask the question that section will answer.
- To motivate a derivation, ask *why* the relationship holds.

Questions are answered immediately or in the following paragraph; they are not left hanging. Write your own questions — do not reuse questions from the input or reference samples.

---

## 4. Stating Laws and Definitions

- **One or two sentences.** State the law or definition clearly and completely before unpacking or deriving.
- **Rephrase in your own words.** Even for well-known laws, write the statement fresh rather than copying any particular textbook's phrasing. The physics must be accurate, but the English must be yours.
- Follow the statement with a short link back to your opening scenario so the reader sees the connection.

---

## 5. Equations: Introduce, Show, Then Explain

- **Introduce in prose** with a short sentence, then place the equation on its own line.
- **Explain notation** in the sentence(s) after the equation: subscripts, sign conventions, and variable meanings.
- **Derivations:** For step-by-step algebra, narrate each step briefly, then show the equation. End with a one-sentence interpretation of what each side means physically.
- **Caveats in prose:** After a theorem, state any conditions or limitations on its use.

Write all surrounding prose in your own words. Standard equation forms (F = ma, KE = ½mv², etc.) are fine to keep, but the explanatory sentences around them must be original.

---

## 6. Misconception Handling

Anticipate and correct common confusions explicitly.

- **Name the mistaken inference** a student is likely to make.
- **Correct it plainly** in one or two direct sentences.
- **Clarify misleading labels** if any standard terminology could create confusion.
- **Give a test or thought experiment** the reader can use to check their own understanding.

All explanations must be in your own words. Do not lift the phrasing of misconception corrections from the input or reference samples.

---

## 7. Structured Procedures and Conditions

When a concept has criteria or steps, make them easy to scan and follow.

- **Number or bullet conditions** so the reader can check them off.
- **Short step lists** for procedures (how to identify something, how to solve a type of problem).
- **Apply the procedure in order** using your own example scenario, walking through each step concretely.

---

## 8. Worked Conceptual Examples

Extend one scenario to test understanding and show how the concept applies.

- Develop a single scenario fully rather than scattering many different examples. Walk the reader through identifying relevant quantities, applying the concept, and interpreting the result.
- **Use a new scenario** — different objects and setup from the input and from the reference samples. The structure (identify → apply → interpret → test) should match, but the content must be original.

---

## 9. Figures and Captions

- **Caption as full sentence(s).** Captions explain what the figure shows and reinforce the concept.
- Write captions in your own words describing your own scenario — do not reuse caption text from the reference samples.
- Reference figures in the body when helpful: "as shown in Figure 2," "[Figure 1]."

---

## 10. Section Headings and Conclusions

- **Clear section headings** that signal content. Write headings that reflect your new scenario and angle, not the headings from the reference samples.
- **Explicit conclusion** for the section, summarizing the key takeaway in one to two sentences. Write this in your own words.
- **Optional closing sentence** that looks forward to how the concept will be used later.

---

## 11. Tone Rules (Physics-Specific)

- **Simultaneity, not cause-and-effect.** For paired forces or simultaneous phenomena, make clear that neither side "causes" the other.
- **Names are arbitrary.** When standard labels could mislead (e.g., "action" vs. "reaction"), state that the labels carry no sequence or priority.
- **Link to prior material.** Reference concepts the reader already knows so they see continuity — but phrase these links in your own words.

---

## 12. Don'ts

- Do not say one force "causes" the other in an action-reaction pair.
- Do not use "action" and "reaction" to imply sequence or cause.
- Do not leave common misconceptions unstated; address them explicitly.
- Do not drop into dense, reference-only prose without a scenario or question to anchor the reader.
- Do not skip the link between the opening scenario and the formal statement.

---

## 13. Output Format Conventions

- Put equations on their own lines; explain variables and conventions in the next sentence(s).
- Use a **Caption:** prefix (or "Figure N. …") for figure captions, as full explanatory sentences.
- Use **Conclusion:** for the final takeaway of a section when you want it to stand out.
- Use lists or short numbered steps for conditions and procedures so structure is easy to see.
