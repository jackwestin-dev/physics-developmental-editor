# Phases of Matter and Density

You pick up an ice cube from a tray and drop it into a glass of water. The ice keeps its shape as it bobs in the liquid. A few minutes later, the cube has vanished—melted into the water. If you leave the glass on the counter for days, the water level drops; the liquid is turning into vapor and escaping into the air. In each case, the same substance is changing how it behaves: sometimes holding a fixed shape, sometimes taking the shape of its container, sometimes filling the whole room. How do we describe these different ways matter behaves?

We classify matter into three common phases, or states: solid, liquid, and gas. You can tell them apart in a straightforward way. A solid keeps a roughly fixed size and shape; changing the volume or shape of a solid usually requires a large force (though a thin object might bend). A liquid has no fixed shape—it takes the shape of its container and can flow—but like a solid it is not easily compressed; its volume changes significantly only under a very large force. A gas has neither a fixed shape nor a fixed volume; it expands to fill its container. When you pump air into a bicycle or car tire, the air doesn’t all sink to the bottom as a liquid would; it spreads out and fills the entire volume of the tire.

Because liquids and gases can flow and do not keep a fixed shape, we refer to them together as fluids.

The division of matter into three phases is not always clear-cut. How, for example, should you classify butter? In addition, a fourth phase—plasma—can be distinguished at very high temperatures; it consists of ionized atoms (electrons separated from nuclei). Some scientists treat colloids (suspensions of tiny particles in a liquid) as a separate phase, and liquid crystals, used in TV, phone, and computer screens, can be thought of as a phase between solid and liquid. For now, we focus on the three ordinary phases: solid, liquid, and gas.

## Density and Specific Gravity

Someone might say that iron is “heavier” than wood. That can’t be right in general—a large log clearly weighs more than a small iron nail. What we really mean is that iron is more dense than wood. Density is the idea that captures “how much stuff per unit volume,” and it is what you need when comparing materials.

The density ρ (the lowercase Greek letter rho) of a substance is defined as its mass per unit volume:

ρ = m / V

Here m is the mass of a sample of the substance and V is its volume. Density is a characteristic property of a pure substance. Objects made of the same pure substance—say, pure gold—can have any size or mass, but the density is the same for each.

From the definition of density, you can write the mass of an object as m = ρV, and the weight as mg = ρVg.

The SI unit for density is kg/m³. Densities are also often given in g/cm³. If a density is given in g/cm³, you must multiply by 1000 to convert to kg/m³, because 1 kg/m³ = 10⁻³ g/cm³. For example, the density of aluminum is ρ = 2.70 g/cm³, which is 2700 kg/m³. Tables of densities usually specify temperature and pressure, because they affect density (the effect is small for liquids and solids). Note that air is about 1000 times less dense than water.

The specific gravity (abbreviated SG) of a substance is the ratio of its density to the density of water at 4.0°C. Because it is a ratio, specific gravity has no dimensions and no units—it is just a number. For example (see Table 10–1), the specific gravity of lead is 11.3, and the SG of alcohol is 0.79.

Density and specific gravity are especially useful when studying fluids, where you are not always dealing with a fixed volume or a fixed mass.

Conclusion: The three common phases of matter—solid, liquid, and gas—differ in whether they keep a fixed shape and volume; liquids and gases are fluids. Density (mass per unit volume) and specific gravity (density relative to water) let you compare materials and analyze fluids without relying on the vague idea that one material is “heavier” than another.
