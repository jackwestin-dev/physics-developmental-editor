When you stand beside a quiet lake, you notice a clear image of yourself gazing back [Figure 1]. Your reflection looks almost as sharp as you are, even though nothing solid lies below the surface. What you are really seeing is how light behaves when it reaches a smooth boundary. Reflection is the process by which incident light rays hit a surface and bounce back into the same medium. 


Figure 1. Reflection of a person and sunlight on the smooth surface of a lake, demonstrating how light bounces back at a boundary between air and water.
Long before we name equations or rules, you’ve already watched the physics of reflection at work every time you look into a mirror, walk across a polished floor, or catch a glint off a car door.
Reflection from a Plane Surface
To make the idea more concrete, let’s bring a small laser pointer and direct it at a flat mirror. A bright beam strikes the surface, and if we slowly adjust the angle of the incoming laser beam, we notice that the departing beam moves with it, as shown in Figure 2.


Figure 2. Incident rays striking a flat mirror surface with corresponding reflected rays, showing the normal line and angles θᵢ and θᵣ measured from the normal.


We call the incoming ray of light the incident ray. The point of incidence is the point where the incident ray reaches the mirror. The normal is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular to a surface at the point of incidence. The reflected ray is the light ray moving away from the surface. The angle of incidence is the angle between the incident ray and the normal. The angle of reflection is the angle between the reflected ray and the normal.
