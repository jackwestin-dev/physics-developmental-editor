Introduction to the Work-Kinetic Energy Theorem
You're riding your bicycle on a flat, straight path through the park, heading to meet your friends at the basketball court. You're coasting along, enjoying the breeze, when you glance at your watch and realize you're running late. You pedal harder, and the bike speeds up. A few minutes later, a dog suddenly darts across the path ahead. You quickly apply the brakes, and the bike slows to a stop just in time.
In both moments, speeding up to make it on time and braking to avoid the dog, forces are doing work on you and your bike. Your speed, and hence, your kinetic energy, change as a result. There's a direct connection between the work done on an object and the change in its kinetic energy, which we call the Work-Kinetic Energy Theorem. The Work-Kinetic Energy Theorem states that the net work done by all forces acting on a system equals the change in its kinetic energy.
Before diving deep into this theorem, we first need to be clear about what we're analyzing: What is our system? And what are the forces that act on it?
Understanding the System and Forces
In physics, we define a system as a portion of the physical universe, consisting of one or more objects, chosen for analysis and separated from its surroundings by a real or imaginary boundary. In our bicycle scenario, the system is you and your bicycle together.

In our analysis using the Work-Kinetic Energy Theorem, we consider only external forces from outside the system. For example, when you pedal a bicycle, your legs push down on the pedals, the chain transfers this force to the rear wheel, and the wheel pushes backward against the ground. By Newton's Third Law, the ground pushes forward on your wheel. This forward push from the ground is an external force acting on our system (you + bicycle), and it's what speeds you up. Your push on the pedals is an internal force that we are not interested in.

Similarly, when you brake, the brake pads squeeze the wheels, and the wheels push against the ground. The ground pushes back, creating a friction force that opposes your motion. This back push from the ground is also an external force.

Other external forces acting on you include:
Air resistance - pushes backward, opposing your motion
Gravity - pulls you downward
Normal force from the ground - pushes you upward

On a flat path, gravity and the normal force are perpendicular to the system’s motion, so they do no work. The forces that matter for changing the system’s speed are the forward force from the ground and the backward resistance.

When we talk about the net force in our following examples, we mean the vector sum of all these external forces.
Why Work Changes Kinetic Energy
You already know that kinetic energy is the energy an object has because of its motion, KE=12mv2, but why does this theorem relate work specifically to kinetic energy rather than to some other form of energy?

Let's derive this connection. Imagine you're riding your bicycle, and an external constant net force Fnet acts on your system as you move forward a distance d. From Newton's Second Law, we know that:
Fnet=ma    a=Fnetm
And there is a kinematic equation that relates the object’s acceleration a over a distance d to its initial and final speeds:
vfinal2=vinitial2+2ad
Substituting a=Fnetm:
vfinal2=vinitial2+2(Fnetm)d
Multiply both sides of the last equation by m:
mvfinal2=mvinitial2+2Fnetd
Now, divide both sides by 2:
12mvfinal2=12mvinitial2+Fnetd
Rearranging:
12mvfinal2-12mvinitial2=Fnetd
The left side shows the change in the object's kinetic energy. The right side is the net (total) work done on that object. So:

Wnet=KE=KEfinal-KEinitial


Note that, since this theorem is derived from Newton’s second law, which deals with the net force acting on an object, the Work-Kinetic Energy Theorem should be used ONLY with the net work done by all forces.
